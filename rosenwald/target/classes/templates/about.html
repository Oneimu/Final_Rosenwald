About Rosenwald School

Julius Rosenwald is “the greatest donor you never heard of,” remarks Peter Ascoli in his biography of this unextraordinary, extra-ordinary man, who also happens to be his grandfather (cited Deutsch ix). Born in 1862, Rosenwald grew up in Springfield, Illinois, across the street from Abraham Lincoln’s home.. In 1895, Rosenwald and his brother in law Aaron Nussbaum purchased Roebuck’s half of Sears and Roebuck after he and his fellow founder parted ways - in 1903, Rosenwald bought Nussbaum out. The company made millions of dollars, and survives to this day - well known to many scholars of American history and culture. But what of Julius Rosenwald, the modest and relatively private businessman whose name was never part of the company nomenclature?

Owing to his Jewish roots, Rosenwald explained, he felt a connection to the discrimination faced by African Americans in the United States. This had been bolstered by his reading of Booker T. Washington’s Up From Slavery. After being introduced to Washington at a fundraising event for a Black YMCA, the two forged a partnership based on improving the condition of African Americans, particularly in the field of education. An “experiment” was launched to build six model schoolhouses in Macon County, Alabama.

In order to maintain both he and Washington’s ideologies of self-help and community involvement, Rosenwald stipulated that he would contribute a set amount of funds to each school -this contribution was never meant to be the largest portion of aid, but rather was meant to act as an incentive, to be supplemented by the local African American and white populations, as well as other local fundraising bodies. Following the success of this trial, the school project expanded, producing architecturally standardized “model” schools across the South. By the end of the Rosenwald School Fund in 1932, the scheme had built almost 5000 schools. Estimates suggest that between one in every three to one in every five African American schools at the end of the Project were funded in part by Julius Rosenwald (others took inspiration from this work, but were not considered official Rosenwald schools). As Mary Hoffschwelle suggests, the Fund turned Rosenwald into “a household name in southern African American communities and an adjective that defined a modern school building” (30). How then, can we get Rosenwald and the important work of his school fund back into the cultural lexicon?

One way in which Rosenwald schools affected the landscape of Southeastern states was through the standardized plans that the Fund created for school design. The Community Schools Plan document featured detailed design figures, and was distributed widely - the Rosenwald Fund decided not to put copyright limitations on the bulletins so that others (white and black) could emulate the plans in their own school building work. Architects at the time saw correlation between specific design features, and the quality of education that could be provided in such buildings. Designers in North Carolina for example, suggested that: “When the room is bright and attractive and the air pure, the scholars are always bright and attentive, and the teachers can do better work. With a poorly lighted room and bad air, the scholars are dull, inattentive and irritable.” (Hoffschwelle 54-55) Schools were usually built so that sunlight would shine into the one wall sided windows, preventing glare for students trying to read and write. Buildings were often rectangular, with hipped roofing and distinctive nine-by-nine pane gallery windows - these hallmarks are part of what keep the structures recognizable to this day. Any proposed Rosenwald school must possess at least two acres of land, to enable gardens and play areas, as well as allowing for possible extension. Furnishings were considered integral to the schoolhouses - desks, blackboards, heat, and toilet facilities had to be considered as part of the building plans. Some schools built kitchens and shops so that they could offer vocational courses and domestic science. The Nashville office of the Fund ran a scorecard scheme, which collated points based on criteria such as if the school had safe drinking water supply, properly constructed roofing, or offered extra curricular activities to the community. Winning schools were often awarded a portrait of Rosenwald as their prize.

In 2002, Rosenwald schools were collectively added to the Most Endangered Historic Sites in America, compiled by the National Trust for Historic Preservation. Individual Rosenwald schools have also appeared independently, including Pine Grove Elementary School, in Virginia, as recently as 2021. The National Trust initiative included a digitization grant, which enabled the Julius Rosenwald School Fund cards and photographs, held at Fisk University Special Collections and Archives, to be compiled in an online database, searchable by state and county, teacher number, budget year, and name. In 2020, Fisk was awarded a Mellon grant to update this database, with plans to move it to a newer content management system that will allow for better searchability and user access, as well as incorporating data visualization. According to Hoffschwelle, “Black southerners viewed Rosenwald schools within a tradition of educational activism,” the idea that education was the key to liberation (247). In fact, this suggestion is borne out by some of the schools’ most famous alumni, including Medgar Evers, John Lewis, and Maya Angelou. Rosa Parks remembered Julius Rosenwald visiting her school in Montgomery. Although the school was not one funded by Rosenwald, “we all knew who he was,” Parks recounted (cited in Deutsch 169). Carlotta Walls LaNier, who attended the Dunbar School, Pulaski, Arkansas went on to become one of the famous Little Rock Nine, integrating Little Rock High School in 1957.
